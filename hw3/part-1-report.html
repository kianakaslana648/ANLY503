Analysis on the Thrombosis Dataset

 

Dataset:

 

The dataset comes from PKDD’99 discovery challenge. It is composed of three parts: tsumoto_a.csv, tsumoto_b.csv, tsumoto_c.csv, which are the records of basic information about patients, results of special laboratory examinations, and common laboratory examinations.

 

Initial Analysis Questions:

 

1. Is there any correlation between the variables of common laboratory examinations?

2. Is there any difference in different groups of patients?

3. Can we find some features from the past examinations of positive patients?

 

Discoveries & Insights:

 

The first thing we’d like to explore is the correlations between different variables.

 

 

While darker and larger circles represent stronger correlation, blank and question marks represent little correlation or no implication for the correlation. We can see that there are strong correlations between pairs of variables such as TP and IGG, PT and DNA. But actually most pairs are weakly related, which lay the foundation for our further analysis.

 

Next, we put our insights into the special laboratory examinations. The above visualization provides a view into antibody levels in different genders. We cannot assert that there’s a difference in the antibody levels between male and female due to lack of samples in males. Actually females take more special laboratory examinations than males.

 

 

 

This plot proves that antibody levels of Negatives are lower than Positives. Furthermore, if we do t-tests for each kind of antibody, it still leads to the same conclusions.

 

 

 

In order to recognize the significance of different variables in special laboratory examinations, we can add the categorical variables and fit a decision tree model. By looking into the structure of the decision tree, we can see the most important variable is LAC.

 

 

 

Finally, we want to explore the past examinations of the patients who are detected positive in the special laboratory examination. While missing values always exist for the reason that patients do not always have each variable measured, we can still create a visualization for the time pattern analysis. It seems that the variables of the past examinations on positive patients are much more correlated, considering what we’ve explored in the first part--most pairs are weakly related. The above picture is a typical one for a positive patient.

 

Conclusions:

 

• Most of the pairs of variables in common laboratory examinations are not strongly related.

• There is no significant difference in antibody levels between males and females.

• It seems that different variables in the past examinations are much related among positive patients.

 

Summary:

 

• A matrix-like plot could provide me with the correlation of different variables.

• A boxplot or a jitter plot with multiple panels could help me dig into the difference between different groups for multiple variables, which is better than the histogram.

• A hierarchy plot could reveal the structure of a decision tree model.

• While multiple-line plots are commonly used to describe the time pattern, in this situation line plots are not suitable because of the gap between examinations as well as the missing values.
